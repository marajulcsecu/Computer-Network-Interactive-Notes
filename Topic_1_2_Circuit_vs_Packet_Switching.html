1. Why Do We Need Switching?

Understanding the fundamental problem that switching solves

🔹 The Core Problem

Imagine the Internet has billions of devices. If we wanted every device to have a direct, dedicated physical connection to every other device, we would need an astronomical number of cables — this is simply impossible. For just 1,000 devices, you'd need about 500,000 individual cables!

The solution? Switching. Instead of connecting every pair of devices directly, we connect all devices to a shared network of switches (or routers) that can forward data from any source to any destination. The question is: how should these switches move data across the network?

There are two fundamentally different approaches:

  1. Circuit Switching — reserve a dedicated path (circuit) before communication begins
  2. Packet Switching — break data into small packets, send each independently
💡 Analogy

Think of it as two ways to travel:
Circuit Switching = Booking a private train compartment. The track is reserved for you the entire journey, even when you're sleeping and not using it.
Packet Switching = Taking a public bus. You share the road with others, you might have to wait, but it's much more efficient when many people want to travel.

2. Circuit Switching

The traditional telephone network approach — dedicate a path before communication

🔹 What is Circuit Switching?

📌 Definition

In circuit switching, a dedicated communication path (called a circuit) is established between the sender and receiver before any data is transmitted. The resources along this path (link bandwidth, switch buffers) are reserved for the duration of the communication session. No other users can use these reserved resources, even if the circuit is idle.

How Does Circuit Switching Work? (3 Phases)

Circuit switching involves three distinct phases:

  1. Connection Setup (Circuit Establishment): Before any data is sent, a dedicated path is established from the sender to the receiver through the network. This involves signaling through all intermediate switches to reserve bandwidth on each link along the path. This takes time — called setup delay.
  2. Data Transfer: Once the circuit is established, data flows continuously along the dedicated path. Since resources are reserved, the data experiences no queuing delay at intermediate switches — it flows smoothly at a guaranteed, constant rate.
  3. Circuit Teardown (Disconnection): After the communication is complete, the circuit is released and the reserved resources become available for other users.
💡 Real-World Example

The traditional telephone network (PSTN — Public Switched Telephone Network) is the classic example of a circuit-switched network. When you make a phone call, a dedicated circuit is established between your phone and the other person's phone. The connection has a guaranteed bandwidth, and you can speak continuously without delay. When you hang up, the circuit is released.

Key Properties of Circuit Switching

  • Dedicated resources: Bandwidth is reserved and guaranteed for the entire duration of the call.
  • No sharing during use: The reserved portion of bandwidth cannot be used by anyone else, even during silent periods.
  • Constant rate: Data flows at a constant, guaranteed rate — ideal for real-time applications like voice calls.
  • Setup delay: There is an initial delay to establish the circuit before data transfer can begin.
  • Waste of resources: If the user is not actively sending data (e.g., silent periods in a phone call), the reserved bandwidth sits idle and wasted.

3. FDM vs. TDM — Multiplexing in Circuit Switching

How multiple circuits share a single physical link

🔹 How Are Circuits Created on a Link?

A single physical link (e.g., a fiber optic cable) can carry multiple circuits simultaneously. This is done through multiplexing — dividing the link's capacity among multiple users. There are two main methods:

📶 FDM (Frequency-Division Multiplexing)

The frequency spectrum of the link is divided into frequency bands. Each circuit gets its own dedicated frequency band. All circuits transmit simultaneously, but on different frequencies.

💡 Analogy

Like multiple radio stations broadcasting at the same time on different frequencies. You tune to one station to hear that particular signal.

Example: A link with 4 kHz bandwidth might be divided into 4 bands of 1 kHz each, allowing 4 simultaneous phone calls. FM radio also uses FDM — each station gets a different frequency.

⏱️ TDM (Time-Division Multiplexing)

Time is divided into frames, and each frame is divided into time slots. Each circuit gets a dedicated time slot in every frame. During its slot, a circuit gets the full bandwidth of the link, but only for that brief duration.

💡 Analogy

Like a group of people taking turns to speak in a round-table discussion. Each person speaks for a fixed time, then the next person speaks.

Example: If there are 4 time slots per frame and the link rate is 1 Mbps, each circuit can transmit at an effective rate of 1 Mbps / 4 = 250 kbps.

📐 Formula: Circuit Rate with TDM
Rate per circuit = Total Link Rate / Number of TDM slots
If a 1.536 Mbps link uses TDM with 24 slots → each circuit gets 1.536 / 24 = 64 kbps
⭐ Key Difference

FDM: Each circuit gets a narrow frequency band all the time.
TDM: Each circuit gets the full bandwidth but only for a short time slot.
In both cases, the total capacity is divided equally — the effective per-circuit rate is the same.

4. Packet Switching

The Internet's approach — break data into packets, send independently

🔹 What is Packet Switching?

📌 Definition

In packet switching, the data to be sent is broken into smaller chunks called packets. Each packet is sent independently through the network — it carries the destination address in its header, and each switch (router) along the way examines this address to decide where to forward the packet next. No dedicated path is reserved — packets from different sources share the same links.

How Does Packet Switching Work?

  1. Packetization: The sending application generates a stream of data. The transport layer breaks this data into packets (small chunks, typically up to ~1,500 bytes each). Each packet gets a header containing the destination IP address and other control information.
  2. Independent forwarding: Each packet enters the network and is forwarded independently by each router it encounters. The router examines the packet's destination address, looks up its forwarding table, and sends the packet out on the appropriate outgoing link.
  3. Statistical multiplexing: Unlike circuit switching where bandwidth is pre-allocated, in packet switching, packets from many different sources share the same link capacity on demand. When a link is free, whoever has a packet ready can use it — this is called statistical multiplexing.
  4. Reassembly: At the destination, packets are reassembled into the original data stream.
💡 Analogy — Postal System

Packet switching is like the postal system: you write a letter, put the destination address on the envelope (= packet header), and drop it in a mailbox. Each post office (= router) along the way reads the address and decides where to send it next. Multiple letters from different people share the same mail trucks and sorting facilities. There is no dedicated route reserved for your letter.

Key Properties of Packet Switching

  • No connection setup: No need to establish a circuit before sending data — packets are sent immediately.
  • Resources are NOT reserved: Links are shared. You get bandwidth only when you have data to send.
  • Store-and-forward: Each router must receive the entire packet before it can begin forwarding it to the next link (see next section).
  • Variable delay: Because packets share links and may have to wait in queues, the delay is variable and unpredictable.
  • Packet loss possible: If a router's buffer (queue) is full when a packet arrives, the packet is dropped (lost).
  • Highly efficient for bursty traffic: Since resources are shared on demand, packet switching can support many more users than circuit switching for bursty applications (like web browsing, where you send data in short bursts).

5. Store-and-Forward Transmission

A critical concept for delay calculations — asked directly in 2023 exam

🔹 What is Store-and-Forward?

📌 Definition

In store-and-forward transmission, the packet switch (router) must receive the entire packet before it can begin transmitting the first bit of the packet onto the outgoing link. The router "stores" the packet in its buffer, and only after the last bit arrives does it "forward" the packet on the next link.

Why Store-and-Forward?

This is necessary because the router needs to:

  • Receive all bits to check for errors (verify the packet is not corrupted)
  • Read the packet header to determine the destination address
  • Look up the forwarding table to decide the outgoing link

Delay Calculation for Store-and-Forward

Consider the simplest case: a packet of L bits being sent over a single link of transmission rate R bits/sec.

📐 Transmission Delay for One Link
dtrans = L / R
L = packet length (in bits)
R = link transmission rate (in bits/sec, i.e., bps)
dtrans = time to push all L bits onto the link

Now consider sending a packet through one switch (router) between the source and destination:

📐 End-to-End Delay with One Switch (Store-and-Forward)
dend-to-end = L/R1 + L/R2
R1 = transmission rate of link from source to switch
R2 = transmission rate of link from switch to destination
First, the entire packet must be received by the switch (L/R1 seconds).
Then, the switch forwards it (L/R2 seconds).
(Ignoring queuing, propagation, and processing delays)

For the general case with N links (i.e., N-1 switches), if all links have the same rate R:

📐 General Formula: N Links, Same Rate R
dend-to-end = N × (L / R)
N = number of links between source and destination
⭐ Critical Exam Point

Store-and-forward means the switch waits for the entire packet before forwarding. This adds a L/R delay at each hop. With different link rates (R₁, R₂, ...), you simply add up L/Ri for each link.

6. Queuing Delay & Packet Loss

What happens when packets arrive faster than they can be forwarded

🔹 Why Does Queuing Happen?

In packet switching, since links are shared and there's no reservation, multiple packets from different sources may arrive at a router at the same time, all wanting to be forwarded on the same outgoing link. The router can only transmit one packet at a time on each link. So what happens to the extra packets?

The router stores the waiting packets in an output buffer (queue). Packets wait in the queue until it's their turn to be transmitted. This waiting time is called queuing delay.

Packet Loss

The output buffer has a finite size. If packets arrive when the buffer is already completely full, the router has no choice but to drop (discard) the arriving packet — this is called packet loss. The lost packet may need to be retransmitted by the sender or by a higher-layer protocol like TCP.

⭐ Key Difference

Circuit Switching: No queuing delay (resources are reserved), no packet loss — but possible waste of bandwidth during idle periods.
Packet Switching: Queuing delay is variable, packet loss can occur — but bandwidth is used efficiently through statistical multiplexing.

8. Advantages & Disadvantages

Pros and cons of each approach

📞 Circuit Switching

✅ Advantages:

  • Guaranteed bandwidth — constant rate
  • No queuing delay during data transfer
  • No packet loss during transfer
  • Ideal for real-time, continuous data (voice)
  • Predictable, constant delay

❌ Disadvantages:

  • Wastes bandwidth during silent/idle periods
  • Connection setup delay before data transfer
  • Inflexible — reserved resources even when not needed
  • Cannot support many users for bursty traffic
  • Dedicated resources even during inactivity

📦 Packet Switching

✅ Advantages:

  • Efficient use of bandwidth — statistical multiplexing
  • Supports more users for bursty traffic
  • No connection setup delay
  • Simpler — no complex circuit setup/teardown
  • More robust — if one link fails, packets can take alternative routes

❌ Disadvantages:

  • No guaranteed bandwidth
  • Variable, unpredictable delay (queuing)
  • Packet loss possible (buffer overflow)
  • Requires more complex protocols for reliability
  • Not ideal for constant-rate, real-time applications without additional mechanisms (like QoS)

9. Important Formulas for Exam

Only the formulas that may come in exam with symbol meanings

🔹 Formula Summary

① Transmission Delay (One Link)
dtrans = L / R
L = packet size in bits  |  R = link transmission rate in bps
② Store-and-Forward Delay — One Switch, Two Links (Different Rates)
dend-to-end = L/R₁ + L/R₂
R₁ = rate of link 1 (source → switch)  |  R₂ = rate of link 2 (switch → destination)
③ Store-and-Forward Delay — N Links, Same Rate R
dend-to-end = N × (L / R)
N = number of links (hops)  |  Valid when all links have the same rate R
④ Circuit Rate with TDM
Rate per circuit = Link Rate / Number of slots
In TDM, total link bandwidth is divided equally among all time slots (circuits)
⑤ Maximum Users in Circuit-Switched Network (TDM)
Max users = Link Rate / Rate per user
Each user gets a fixed rate; the total number is determined by the link capacity

10. Numerical Examples (Step-by-Step)

Practice problems with complete solutions

🔹 Example 1: Store-and-Forward Delay

Problem: There is exactly one packet switch between a sending host and a receiving host. The transmission rate between the sending host and the switch is R₁ = 2 Mbps, and between the switch and the receiving host is R₂ = 1 Mbps. The packet length is L = 10,000 bits. Assuming store-and-forward switching, what is the total end-to-end delay? (Ignore queuing, propagation, and processing delays.)

Step 1: Identify the given values

L = 10,000 bits
R₁ = 2 Mbps = 2 × 10⁶ bps
R₂ = 1 Mbps = 1 × 10⁶ bps

Step 2: Apply the store-and-forward formula
dend-to-end = L/R₁ + L/R₂
Step 3: Calculate each term

L/R₁ = 10,000 / (2 × 10⁶) = 0.005 seconds = 5 ms

L/R₂ = 10,000 / (1 × 10⁶) = 0.01 seconds = 10 ms

Step 4: Total delay
dend-to-end = 5 ms + 10 ms = 15 ms

🔹 Example 2: Circuit Switching — Capacity Problem

Problem: A link has a transmission rate of 1.536 Mbps. It uses TDM with 24 time slots. (a) What is the rate per circuit? (b) How many simultaneous calls can this link support?

Step 1: Given

Total link rate = 1.536 Mbps
Number of TDM slots = 24

Step 2: Rate per circuit
Rate per circuit = 1.536 Mbps / 24 = 64 kbps
Step 3: Maximum simultaneous calls
Max calls = 24 (one per slot)

Each time slot is one circuit, so the link supports exactly 24 simultaneous calls.

11. Store-and-Forward vs. Cut-Through Switching

Asked in 2019 exam — a related comparison within packet switching

🔹 Two Approaches Within Packet Switching

Within packet-switched networks, there are two techniques a switch can use to forward packets:

FeatureStore-and-ForwardCut-Through
How it worksSwitch waits to receive the entire packet, then forwards itSwitch begins forwarding the packet as soon as it reads the destination address in the header (before receiving the full packet)
LatencyHigher — must wait for full packetLower — forwarding starts almost immediately
Error DetectionCan check the entire packet for errors (CRC check) before forwarding — damaged frames are NOT forwardedCannot check for errors before forwarding — damaged frames ARE forwarded
Key AdvantagePrevents damaged/corrupted packets from being forwarded into the networkLower delay, faster forwarding
Used byMost Internet routersSome high-speed LAN switches
⭐ Exam Point (2019)

The main advantage of store-and-forward over cut-through is that store-and-forward can detect and discard damaged (corrupted) frames before forwarding them. In cut-through switching, since forwarding begins before the entire frame is received, the switch cannot check for errors — and damaged frames get passed along, wasting network bandwidth.

12. Key Points Summary for Exam

Quick-revision checklist

🔹 Must-Remember Points

  1. Circuit Switching = reserve a dedicated path before communication (3 phases: setup, transfer, teardown). Example: telephone network.
  2. Packet Switching = break data into packets, send independently, no reserved path. Example: the Internet.
  3. FDM divides the link by frequency; TDM divides by time. Both are used in circuit switching.
  4. Circuit switching guarantees bandwidth but wastes resources during idle periods.
  5. Packet switching is more efficient for bursty traffic (web browsing) but has variable delay and possible packet loss.
  6. Store-and-forward: switch receives entire packet before forwarding → delay = L/R per link.
  7. With one switch and two links: delay = L/R₁ + L/R₂.
  8. Statistical multiplexing = sharing resources on demand → allows more users than circuit switching.
  9. For steady-rate, long-running applications, circuit switching is more appropriate.
  10. Store-and-forward detects damaged frames before forwarding; cut-through does not.
  11. Packet switching uses the Internet's "best-effort" model — no guarantees on delay, loss, or bandwidth.
  12. In circuit switching, if congestion occurs, new calls are blocked. In packet switching, congestion leads to queuing and loss.

13. Previous Year Exam Questions & Model Answers

All questions from 2017–2023 related to Circuit/Packet Switching
2023 Q1.b) Suppose there is exactly one packet switch between a sending host and a receiving host. The transmission rates between the sending host and the switch and between the switch and the receiving host are R₁ and R₂, respectively. Assuming that the switch uses store-and-forward packet switching, what is the total end-to-end delay to send a packet of length L? (Ignore queuing, propagation delay, and processing delay.) 3 Marks

Model Answer

In store-and-forward packet switching, the switch must receive the entire packet before it can begin to transmit the packet onto the next link. This means:

Step 1: The source host transmits the packet of length L bits onto the first link at rate R₁ bps. The time for the entire packet to arrive at the switch is:

Time to reach switch = L / R₁ seconds

Step 2: Once the switch has received the entire packet, it begins transmitting the packet onto the second link at rate R₂ bps. The time for the packet to be transmitted from the switch to the receiving host is:

Time from switch to receiver = L / R₂ seconds

Step 3: Since the switch must wait for the entire packet before forwarding, these two delays occur sequentially (one after the other). Therefore, the total end-to-end delay is:

dend-to-end = L/R₁ + L/R₂

Explanation: This delay consists of two transmission delays added together. The first term (L/R₁) is the time for the sending host to push all L bits onto the first link. Only after the switch has received all L bits (store phase) does it start to forward them on the second link (forward phase), which takes L/R₂ seconds. Since we are ignoring propagation, queuing, and processing delays, the total end-to-end delay is simply the sum of these two transmission delays.

2023 Q1.c) Consider an application that transmits data at a steady rate (the sender generates an N-bit unit of data every k time unit). (i) Would a packet-switched or circuit-switched network be more appropriate? (ii) If packet-switched is used and total data rate < link capacity, is congestion control needed? 3 Marks

Model Answer

(i) Circuit-switched network would be more appropriate.

A circuit-switched network would be more suitable for this application for the following reasons:

  • The application transmits data at a steady, constant rate (N bits every k time units) — this is exactly the kind of traffic circuit switching was designed for.
  • In circuit switching, a dedicated circuit with guaranteed, constant bandwidth is allocated to the user. Since the application generates data at a constant rate, the circuit's reserved bandwidth would be fully utilized — there would be minimal or no waste of resources.
  • The application runs for a long period of time, which justifies the overhead of the initial connection setup. The setup delay is insignificant compared to the long duration of the communication.
  • With circuit switching, the steady-rate data would experience no queuing delay and no packet loss, ensuring smooth delivery.

In contrast, a packet-switched network would not guarantee constant bandwidth, and the data might experience variable delays and potential loss — which is unnecessary when the traffic pattern is steady and predictable.

(ii) No, congestion control would NOT be needed.

The problem states that the sum of all application data rates is less than the capacity of each and every link in the network. This is a critical condition. Congestion occurs when the total traffic demand exceeds the capacity of links, causing queues to build up at routers. Since the total traffic from all applications is less than the link capacities:

  • No link will become a bottleneck.
  • There will be essentially no queuing delay at any router.
  • There will be no packet loss due to buffer overflow.

Therefore, there is no congestion, and no form of congestion control is needed in this specific scenario. The packet-switched network would behave almost like a circuit-switched network — with minimal delay and no loss.

2018 Q1.d) What advantage does a circuit-switched network have over a packet-switched network? 1 Mark

Model Answer

The main advantage of a circuit-switched network over a packet-switched network is that it provides a guaranteed, constant rate of transmission. Once a circuit is established, the sender is allocated a dedicated portion of the link's bandwidth for the entire duration of the communication session.

This means:

  • The data experiences no queuing delay at intermediate switches — it flows smoothly and continuously.
  • There is no packet loss due to buffer overflow.
  • The end-to-end delay is constant and predictable, which is critical for real-time applications like traditional telephone calls.

In a packet-switched network, there is no bandwidth guarantee — packets may experience variable queuing delays and may even be dropped if router buffers overflow.

2022 Q2.b) Suppose end system A wants to send a large file to end system B. At a very high level, describe how end system A creates packets from the file. When one of these packets arrives at a router, what information in the packet does the router use to determine the link onto which the packet is forwarded? Why is packet switching in the Internet analogous to driving from one city to another and asking directions along the way? 3 Marks

Model Answer

How end system A creates packets:

End system A takes the large file and breaks it into smaller chunks of data. To each chunk, it adds a header containing control information — most importantly the destination IP address of end system B. This combination of header + data chunk forms a packet. The packets are then sent one by one into the network through the access link.

What information does the router use to forward the packet?

When a packet arrives at a router, the router examines the destination IP address in the packet's header. It then looks up this destination address in its forwarding table (a table that maps destination addresses or address prefixes to outgoing links). Based on this lookup, the router determines the appropriate outgoing link and forwards the packet onto that link toward the destination.

Why is packet switching analogous to asking directions while driving?

In packet switching, each packet carries the destination address and is forwarded hop-by-hop — at each router, the packet "asks" (via its header) where to go next, and the router tells it which link to take. This is analogous to driving from one city to another without a predetermined route. At each intersection (router), you stop and ask a local person (consult the forwarding table) for directions to the next town. You do not have a complete route planned in advance — you discover the route incrementally, one hop at a time.

In contrast, circuit switching would be like getting a complete, pre-planned route from a GPS before starting the journey — the entire path is known and reserved in advance.

2019 Q4.b) Store-and-forward switches have an advantage over cut-through switches with respect to damaged frames. Explain what it is. 2 Marks

Model Answer

In a store-and-forward switch, the switch waits to receive the entire frame before it begins to forward it onto the outgoing link. This means the switch has the complete frame in its buffer, including the error-detection field (such as the CRC — Cyclic Redundancy Check) at the end of the frame.

Before forwarding, the switch can compute a checksum on the received frame and compare it with the CRC value in the frame. If the frame has been damaged (corrupted) during transmission — for example, due to electromagnetic interference on the wire — the checksum will not match, and the switch will discard the damaged frame instead of forwarding it. This prevents corrupted data from propagating through the network, saving bandwidth on downstream links.

In contrast, a cut-through switch begins forwarding the frame as soon as it has read just the destination address in the header — it does not wait for the entire frame to arrive. This means the switch has no opportunity to check for errors before forwarding. If the frame is damaged, the cut-through switch will forward the corrupted frame onto the network. The error will only be detected at the final destination, and the damaged frame would have wasted bandwidth on all intermediate links.

Conclusion: The advantage of store-and-forward switches is that they can detect and discard damaged frames before forwarding, preventing corrupted data from consuming bandwidth on subsequent links.

2017 Q1.a.vii) Techniques to switch packets over network. Part of 2.75 Marks

Model Answer

There are two main techniques to switch data over a network:

1. Circuit Switching:

In circuit switching, a dedicated communication path (circuit) is established between the sender and receiver before data transmission begins. The resources (link bandwidth) along this path are reserved for the entire duration of the communication. Data flows continuously at a guaranteed rate with no queuing delay. The circuit is released after the communication ends. The traditional telephone network (PSTN) is the primary example. Circuit switching uses FDM (Frequency Division Multiplexing) or TDM (Time Division Multiplexing) to create multiple circuits on a single link.

2. Packet Switching:

In packet switching, the data is broken into small packets. Each packet carries the destination address in its header and is sent independently through the network. Routers along the path use forwarding tables to determine the next hop for each packet. No dedicated path is reserved — resources are shared among all users through statistical multiplexing. This means multiple packets from different sources share the same links on demand. The Internet is the primary example of a packet-switched network. Packet switching is more efficient for bursty traffic but offers no bandwidth guarantees, and packets may experience variable delays and possible loss.

Additionally, within packet switching, switches can use either:

  • Store-and-forward: The switch receives the entire packet before forwarding (used by most routers).
  • Cut-through: The switch begins forwarding as soon as the destination address is read (lower latency but cannot detect damaged frames).
