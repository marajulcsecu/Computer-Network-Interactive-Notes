1. Overview — Types of Delay

Understanding what causes delay when a packet travels through the network

🔹 The Journey of a Packet

When a packet travels from a source host to a destination host through a packet-switched network (like the Internet), it passes through a series of routers. At each router along the path, the packet suffers several types of delay. Understanding these delays is critical because they determine the overall performance of the network.

There are four types of delay that a packet experiences at each router (node):

🔍

1. Processing Delay (dproc)

Time for router to examine the packet header and determine where to forward it.

🚦

2. Queuing Delay (dqueue)

Time the packet waits in the output buffer before being transmitted.

📤

3. Transmission Delay (dtrans)

Time to push all the packet's bits onto the outgoing link.

🌊

4. Propagation Delay (dprop)

Time for a bit to travel across the physical link from one end to the other.

📐 Nodal Delay (Total Delay at One Router)
dnodal = dproc + dqueue + dtrans + dprop
The total delay at a single node (router) is the sum of all four delay components.
🔗

2. Nodal Delay (dnodal)

The total delay a packet experiences at a single router

🔹 What is Nodal Delay?

📌 Definition

The nodal delay is the total delay that a packet accumulates at a single node (router) as it is processed, waits in a queue, is transmitted onto the outgoing link, and propagates to the next node.

Think of a packet arriving at a router like a traveler arriving at a highway toll plaza:

  • Processing = the toll booth operator checking your ticket (reading the destination)
  • Queuing = waiting in line behind other cars
  • Transmission = the time it takes for your entire car to pass through the booth gate
  • Propagation = driving the distance from this toll plaza to the next one

3. Processing Delay (dproc)

The time a router takes to examine the packet header

🔹 What is Processing Delay?

📌 Definition

The processing delay is the time required for a router to examine the packet's header, determine the output link based on the forwarding table lookup, and check for bit-level errors in the packet.

What happens during processing?

  • Header examination: The router reads the destination IP address from the packet header.
  • Forwarding table lookup: It matches the destination IP against its forwarding table to decide which output link to send the packet on.
  • Error checking: It may check for bit errors that might have occurred during transmission on the incoming link.

Characteristics

  • Magnitude: Typically on the order of microseconds (μs) or less in modern high-speed routers.
  • Type: Constant — generally the same for every packet at a given router (depends on router hardware speed).
  • Often negligible in calculations compared to other delays.
🚦

4. Queuing Delay (dqueue)

Waiting in line — the most complex and variable delay

🔹 What is Queuing Delay?

📌 Definition

The queuing delay is the time a packet spends waiting in the router's output buffer (queue) before it can be transmitted onto the outgoing link. This happens when other packets are already queued for transmission on that same link.

When Does Queuing Happen?

When the router needs to send a packet out on a particular link, but that link is already busy transmitting another packet, the new packet must wait. It joins a queue (line) in the output buffer. The longer the queue, the longer the wait.

💡 Analogy

Queuing delay is like waiting at a supermarket checkout. If you arrive and no one is ahead of you, the wait is zero. If 10 people are in line, you must wait for all of them to be served. The wait depends entirely on how busy the store is.

Characteristics

  • Magnitude: Ranges from zero (empty queue) to milliseconds (long queue) — can be very large during congestion.
  • Type: Variable — depends on the intensity of traffic arriving at the router. This is the most unpredictable delay component.
  • Traffic Intensity: A key metric that determines queuing behavior is:
📐 Traffic Intensity
Traffic Intensity = La / R
L = average packet length (bits)
a = average packet arrival rate (packets/sec)
R = link transmission rate (bps)
La = average rate at which bits arrive at the queue
R = rate at which bits are pushed out of the queue
  • If La/R > 1 → bits arrive faster than they leave → queue grows without bound → packet loss!
  • If La/R ≤ 1 → depends on arrival pattern. If bursty, queuing may still build up temporarily.
  • If La/R ≈ 0 → almost no queuing delay.
⭐ Golden Rule

Design your system so that traffic intensity La/R < 1. If traffic intensity approaches or exceeds 1, queuing delays become extremely large and packet loss is inevitable.

📤

5. Transmission Delay (dtrans)

The time to push all bits of a packet onto the link

🔹 What is Transmission Delay?

📌 Definition

The transmission delay is the amount of time required for the router to push (transmit) all the bits of the packet onto the outgoing link. It is the time from when the first bit of the packet is placed on the link until the last bit of the packet is placed on the link.

Intuition

Imagine you have a line of 1,000 toy soldiers (= bits) and you need to load them one-by-one onto a conveyor belt. The speed at which you can place soldiers on the belt is the transmission rate (R). The total time to load all soldiers is the transmission delay.

The transmission delay depends on two things:

  1. The size of the packet (L): More bits = more time to push them all out.
  2. The transmission rate of the link (R): A faster link pushes bits out faster.
📐 Formula: Transmission Delay
dtrans = L / R
L = length of the packet (in bits)
R = transmission rate of the outgoing link (in bits per second, bps)
dtrans = time to transmit the packet (in seconds)

Characteristics

  • Magnitude: Depends on packet size and link speed. For a 1,500-byte packet on a 1 Gbps Ethernet link: (1500 × 8) / (10⁹) = 12 μs. On a 56 kbps dial-up: (1500 × 8) / 56000 ≈ 214 ms.
  • Type: Constant for a given packet size and link rate — does not vary with traffic.
⚠️ Common Confusion

Transmission delay is NOT about how far the bit has to travel. It's about how long it takes to load all the bits onto the link. It depends on packet size and link speed, NOT on distance.

🌊

6. Propagation Delay (dprop)

The time for a bit to travel across the physical link

🔹 What is Propagation Delay?

📌 Definition

The propagation delay is the time it takes for a single bit to travel (propagate) from one end of the link to the other end. It is the time for the electromagnetic or optical signal to physically travel the length of the wire or fiber.

Intuition

Once a bit is placed on the link (by the transmitter), it travels through the physical medium (copper wire, fiber optic cable, or air) at a speed close to the speed of light. The propagation delay depends on two things:

  1. The distance (d): The physical length of the link. Longer link = more time.
  2. The propagation speed (s): How fast the signal travels through the medium. In copper wire, it's about 2 × 10⁸ m/s. In fiber optic, it's about 2 × 10⁸ to 3 × 10⁸ m/s.
📐 Formula: Propagation Delay
dprop = d / s
d = distance between two routers (in meters)
s = propagation speed of the medium (in meters per second, m/s)
dprop = propagation delay (in seconds)

Typical propagation speeds: Copper ≈ 2.3 × 10⁸ m/s, Fiber ≈ 2 × 10⁸ m/s, Air ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s

Characteristics

  • Magnitude: Depends entirely on the physical distance. For a short LAN cable (100m): negligible. For an intercontinental link (10,000 km via fiber): d_prop = 10⁷ / (2 × 10⁸) = 50 ms.
  • Type: Constant — for a given physical link, the distance and speed don't change.
⚠️ Common Confusion

Propagation delay depends on distance and medium speed — NOT on packet size or link transmission rate. Even if you double the link speed, the propagation delay stays the same.

⚖️

7. Transmission Delay vs. Propagation Delay

The most commonly confused pair — understand the difference clearly

🔹 Key Comparison

This is one of the most important distinctions in networking. Many students confuse these two delays, and exams often test this distinction.

FeatureTransmission Delay (dtrans)Propagation Delay (dprop)
What it measuresTime to push ALL bits of the packet onto the linkTime for ONE bit to travel the full length of the link
Formuladtrans = L / Rdprop = d / s
Depends onPacket size (L) and link rate (R)Link distance (d) and propagation speed (s)
Independent ofDistance between routersPacket size or link speed
AnalogyTime to load all cars of a train onto a highway on-rampTime for the first car to drive from one city to another
Where it occursAt the transmitting end of the linkAcross the link (in the wire/fiber/air)
💡 Caravan Analogy (from Kurose textbook)

Imagine a caravan of 10 cars traveling from one toll booth to the next. The toll booth can process (push out) 1 car every 12 seconds.

  • Transmission delay = time to push all 10 cars through the toll booth = 10 × 12 = 120 seconds. This depends on the number of cars (packet length) and the booth speed (link rate).
  • Propagation delay = time for one car to drive from this toll booth to the next one (say 100 km at 100 km/hr = 1 hour). This depends on distance and speed of the car on the highway.

These are completely independent! Making the toll booth faster (higher R) doesn't make the highway shorter (lower d). Making the highway shorter doesn't make the toll booth faster.

⭐ Memory Aid

Transmission = "loading time" — depends on how much data (L) and how fast you can push (R).
Propagation = "travel time" — depends on how far (d) and how fast the signal moves (s).

8. End-to-End Delay

Total delay from source host to destination host

🔹 Calculating the Total End-to-End Delay

The end-to-end delay is the total delay a packet experiences from the moment it leaves the source host until it arrives at the destination host. If the path goes through N links (i.e., N-1 routers), and assuming no queuing delay:

📐 End-to-End Delay (N links, homogeneous)
dend-to-end = N × (dproc + dtrans + dprop)
Assuming all N links have the same transmission rate, length, and processing delay, and queuing delay is negligible.

For the simplest case where we only consider transmission and propagation delay (ignoring processing and queuing):

📐 Simplified End-to-End Delay (One Link)
dend-to-end = dtrans + dprop = L/R + d/s
This is the expression asked in the 2017 exam Q1.c.iii.
❌

9. Packet Loss

What happens when the buffer overflows

🔹 Why Do Packets Get Lost?

📌 Definition

Packet loss occurs when a packet arrives at a router whose output buffer (queue) is completely full. Since there is no room to store the arriving packet, the router drops (discards) it. The packet is lost and must be retransmitted.

Key points about packet loss:

  • Packet loss is a direct consequence of finite buffer size and congestion.
  • As traffic intensity (La/R) approaches 1, the fraction of lost packets increases.
  • Lost packets may be retransmitted by the sending host (e.g., TCP retransmits lost segments), or they may simply be lost (e.g., UDP does not retransmit).
  • Packet loss is a performance issue — it reduces effective throughput and can cause delays due to retransmissions.
⭐ Exam Point

The performance of a node is often measured by delay, loss, and throughput. Packet loss occurs because router buffers have finite capacity. When the buffer is full, arriving packets are dropped.

10. Throughput & Bottleneck Links

How fast data actually flows — a critical exam concept

🔹 What is Throughput?

📌 Definition

Throughput is the rate (in bits per second) at which data is actually transferred between the sender and receiver. It is the effective data transfer rate as seen by the application.

There are two types of throughput:

  • Instantaneous throughput: The rate at a given instant in time (e.g., what your download manager shows as current speed).
  • Average throughput: If you transfer F bits of a file in T seconds, then average throughput = F/T bps.

What is a Bottleneck Link?

📌 Definition

When data travels through a path consisting of multiple links with different transmission rates, the bottleneck link is the link with the lowest transmission rate on the path. The end-to-end throughput is limited by this bottleneck link.

📐 Formula: End-to-End Throughput
Throughput = min{R₁, R₂, R₃, ..., RN}
The throughput of a path is the minimum of the transmission rates of all links along the path. The link with the minimum rate is the bottleneck link.
💡 Analogy — Water Pipes

Think of data flowing through a series of water pipes of different diameters. The amount of water that flows through the entire pipeline is limited by the narrowest pipe — no matter how wide the other pipes are. That narrowest pipe is the "bottleneck."

Transfer Time

If you know the throughput and the file size, you can calculate the transfer time:

📐 File Transfer Time
Time = F / Throughput = F / min{R₁, R₂, ..., RN}
F = file size (in bits)  |  Throughput = bottleneck rate (in bps)
⭐ Key Exam Concept

The end-to-end throughput is always limited by the bottleneck link — the link with the smallest transmission rate along the path. In today's Internet, the bottleneck is often the access network (your home DSL/Cable connection), not the core.

12. Numerical Examples (Step-by-Step)

Practice solving problems with complete working

🔹 Example 1: Propagation & Transmission Delay

Problem: Two hosts A and B are connected by a single link of rate R = 10 Mbps. They are separated by m = 5,000 km. Propagation speed is s = 2.5 × 10⁸ m/s. Host A sends a packet of L = 1,500 bytes. Find dprop, dtrans, and the end-to-end delay.

Step 1: Convert units

m = 5,000 km = 5 × 10⁶ m
L = 1,500 bytes = 1,500 × 8 = 12,000 bits
R = 10 Mbps = 10 × 10⁶ bps

Step 2: Propagation Delay
dprop = d / s = (5 × 10⁶) / (2.5 × 10⁸) = 0.02 s = 20 ms
Step 3: Transmission Delay
dtrans = L / R = 12,000 / (10 × 10⁶) = 0.0012 s = 1.2 ms
Step 4: End-to-End Delay (ignoring processing & queuing)
de2e = dtrans + dprop = 1.2 ms + 20 ms = 21.2 ms
💡 Observation

In this case, propagation delay (20 ms) is much larger than transmission delay (1.2 ms). This is typical for long-distance links with high transmission rates — the distance dominates.

🔹 Example 2: Throughput & Transfer Time

Problem: Host A sends a 4 million byte file to Host B. The path has three links: R₁ = 500 kbps, R₂ = 2 Mbps, R₃ = 1 Mbps. (a) What is the throughput? (b) How long to transfer the file?

Step 1: Find the bottleneck (throughput)
Throughput = min{R₁, R₂, R₃} = min{500 kbps, 2 Mbps, 1 Mbps} = 500 kbps

The bottleneck link is R₁ = 500 kbps (the slowest link).

Step 2: Convert file size to bits
F = 4 million bytes = 4 × 10⁶ × 8 = 32 × 10⁶ bits = 32 Mbits
Step 3: Transfer time
Time = F / Throughput = (32 × 10⁶) / (500 × 10³) = 64 seconds

It takes approximately 64 seconds to transfer the file.

13. Key Points Summary for Exam

Quick-revision checklist

🔹 Must-Remember Points

  1. There are 4 types of delay: processing, queuing, transmission, propagation.
  2. dtrans = L/R depends on packet size and link rate (NOT distance).
  3. dprop = d/s depends on distance and medium speed (NOT packet size or link rate).
  4. Don't confuse transmission and propagation! Transmission = "loading time," Propagation = "travel time."
  5. Processing delay = constant, microseconds. Queuing delay = variable, can be huge.
  6. If traffic intensity La/R ≥ 1, queue grows without bound → packet loss.
  7. Packet loss occurs when router buffers (finite size) overflow.
  8. Throughput = min{R₁, R₂, ...} — limited by the bottleneck link (slowest link).
  9. Transfer time = F / Throughput.
  10. Constant delays: processing, transmission, propagation. Variable delay: queuing.
  11. In modern Internet, the access network (home DSL/Cable) is typically the bottleneck.
  12. End-to-end delay for one link (simplified) = L/R + d/s.

14. Previous Year Exam Questions & Model Answers

All questions from 2017–2023 related to Topic 1.3
2022 Q2.a) Consider sending a packet from a source host to a destination host over a fixed route. List the delay components in the end-to-end delay. Which of these delays are constant and which are variable? 2+1 = 3 Marks

Model Answer

When a packet is sent from a source to a destination over a fixed route, it passes through a series of routers. At each router (node), the packet experiences four components of delay. These four components together form the nodal delay:

dnodal = dproc + dqueue + dtrans + dprop

The four delay components are:

1. Processing Delay (dproc): The time required for the router to examine the packet's header, determine the output link for forwarding (via forwarding table lookup), and check for bit-level errors. This is typically on the order of microseconds.

2. Queuing Delay (dqueue): The time the packet waits in the router's output buffer (queue) before being transmitted onto the outgoing link. This depends on the intensity of traffic — if many packets are already waiting, the queuing delay is large.

3. Transmission Delay (dtrans = L/R): The time required for the router to push all L bits of the packet onto the outgoing link, where R is the transmission rate of the link in bps.

4. Propagation Delay (dprop = d/s): The time for a bit to physically travel (propagate) across the link from the router to the next node, where d is the link length and s is the propagation speed of the medium.

Classification — Constant vs. Variable:

Delay ComponentTypeReason
Processing Delay (dproc)ConstantDepends on router hardware speed, which doesn't change with traffic
Queuing Delay (dqueue)VariableDepends on the amount of traffic — varies from zero to very large depending on congestion
Transmission Delay (dtrans)ConstantDepends only on fixed values: packet size L and link rate R
Propagation Delay (dprop)ConstantDepends only on fixed values: link distance d and propagation speed s

Conclusion: Three of the four delay components (processing, transmission, propagation) are constant for a given network configuration. Only the queuing delay is variable, as it depends on the current traffic load at the router.

2017 Q1.c) Two hosts A and B are connected by a single link of rate R bps. They are separated by m meters, propagation speed is s m/s. Host A sends a packet of L bits.
i. Express d_prop in terms of m and s.
ii. Determine d_trans in terms of L and R.
iii. Obtain expression for end-to-end delay.
iv. At t = d_trans, where is the last bit?
v. If d_prop > d_trans, at t = d_trans, where is the first bit? Part of combined marks

Model Answer

(i) Propagation delay in terms of m and s:

dprop = m / s

The propagation delay is the time for a bit to travel the physical distance m of the link, at the propagation speed s of the medium.

(ii) Transmission time in terms of L and R:

dtrans = L / R

The transmission delay is the time for Host A to push all L bits of the packet onto the link, at the transmission rate R.

(iii) End-to-end delay (ignoring processing and queuing):

dend-to-end = dtrans + dprop = L/R + m/s

The total delay is the sum of the transmission delay (time to put all bits on the link) and the propagation delay (time for bits to travel the distance).

(iv) At time t = dtrans, where is the last bit of the packet?

At time t = dtrans, Host A has just finished transmitting the packet — the last bit has just been placed onto the link. Therefore, the last bit is at Host A's end of the link (i.e., at the beginning of the link, just leaving the transmitter). It has not yet started propagating.

(v) If dprop > dtrans, at time t = dtrans, where is the first bit?

The first bit was placed on the link at time t = 0. It has been propagating for dtrans seconds. Since dprop > dtrans, the first bit has NOT yet reached Host B — it is still somewhere on the link, between Host A and Host B.

Specifically, the first bit has traveled a distance of s × dtrans = s × (L/R) meters from Host A. Since dprop > dtrans, this distance is less than m (the full link length), confirming the first bit is still in transit on the link.

2019 / 2020 Q2.a / Q1.b) Host A sends a large file to Host B. Path has three links: R₁ = 500 kbps, R₂ = 2 Mbps, R₃ = 1 Mbps.
(i) What is the throughput?
(ii) File is 4 million bytes. How long to transfer?
(iii) Repeat with R₂ = 100 kbps. 3 Marks

Model Answer

(i) Throughput for the file transfer:

The end-to-end throughput is determined by the bottleneck link — the link with the lowest transmission rate along the path.

Given links

R₁ = 500 kbps, R₂ = 2 Mbps = 2,000 kbps, R₃ = 1 Mbps = 1,000 kbps

Find bottleneck
Throughput = min{R₁, R₂, R₃} = min{500, 2000, 1000} kbps = 500 kbps

The bottleneck link is R₁ = 500 kbps.

(ii) Time to transfer the file:

Convert file size

F = 4 million bytes = 4 × 10⁶ bytes = 4 × 10⁶ × 8 bits = 32 × 10⁶ bits

Calculate transfer time
Time = F / Throughput = (32 × 10⁶) / (500 × 10³) = 64 seconds

(iii) Repeat with R₂ reduced to 100 kbps:

New throughput

R₁ = 500 kbps, R₂ = 100 kbps, R₃ = 1 Mbps = 1,000 kbps

Throughput = min{500, 100, 1000} = 100 kbps

Now the bottleneck is R₂ = 100 kbps.

New transfer time
Time = (32 × 10⁶) / (100 × 10³) = 320 seconds ≈ 5.33 minutes
💡 Observation

Reducing R₂ from 2 Mbps to 100 kbps changed the bottleneck from R₁ to R₂, and increased the transfer time from 64 seconds to 320 seconds — a 5× increase, because the new bottleneck is 5× slower than the old one.

2023 Q1.b) There is exactly one packet switch between sender and receiver. Transmission rates are R₁ and R₂. Using store-and-forward, what is the total end-to-end delay for a packet of length L? (Ignore queuing, propagation, processing delay.) 3 Marks

Model Answer

In store-and-forward packet switching, the switch must receive the entire packet before it begins forwarding it onto the next link.

The packet travels over two links:

  • Link 1 (sender → switch): rate = R₁. Time to transmit entire packet = L/R₁
  • Link 2 (switch → receiver): rate = R₂. Time to transmit entire packet = L/R₂

Since the switch must first completely receive the packet (L/R₁ seconds), and then transmit it (L/R₂ seconds), these two delays occur sequentially:

dend-to-end = L/R₁ + L/R₂

This is the total end-to-end delay when ignoring queuing, propagation, and processing delays. It consists of two successive transmission delays — one at the source and one at the switch.
